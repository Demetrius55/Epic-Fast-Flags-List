<h1 align="center">The Ethics of FastFlags Exploitation <sup>by Kuto, owner of EG Prison Life</sup></h3>

<h3 align="center">In recent days, there is much controversy surrounding what has been termed ‘fastflags’. For a short time the RGC (Roblox Glitch Community Discord server) kept it secret, for they believed it too great of a glitch to be allowed to leak. Two days ago as I write, ‘fastflags’ was finally made available to the public, once again, or at least to those who join the RGC server. What is called ‘fastflags’ is more accurately called ‘fastflags exploit’ or ‘fastflags exploitation’, although there has also arrived the term ‘fastflagging’. The reason why this is so is because the term fastflags refers to something else entirely. According to evaera, “The Roblox engine uses a system called Fast Flags as part of its deployment process. When code is shipped, not all of it is active by default. Rather, the changes are suppressed by flags that are dynamically enabled and disabled, even after the code is live on production.” In other words fastflags is the intermediary between the Roblox engine and the Roblox client. The ‘fastflags exploit’ takes advantage of a flaw that Roblox made and essentially allows the player to modify the values of fastflags by editing and creating files in the app. It was obviously never intended, but the fact that it is unintended is why many have come to view it as a glitch by virtue of some definitions of ‘glitch’, in contrast with mere observation which would tell you that it is likely an ‘exploit’ (‘exploit’ here refers to DDL injection and script execution). As a result, it has the appearance of both a glitch and an exploit simultaneously, for it on one hand seems to fall under the definition of ‘glitch’ and on the other hand seems for the outside observer to appear to be an ‘exploit’. It would be more accurate to call it neither a glitch nor an exploit, even though it may sometimes seem like it is one or the other according to your frame of reference.
</h3>

<h3 align="center">While it is obvious that it is not an ‘exploit’ (that is to say, DDL injection) for the person using it, it is trickier to show that it is not a glitch because it involves trying to overthrow assumptions that people have about the nature of things. There are two facts which I would like to underline and then combine. The first is that the definition of ‘glitch’ is very complex. When defining what a glitch is it is frequent for people to define it by parts of its nature. For instance, they will refer to the idea of a glitch being a flaw, and while that is correct, it is not the entire story. Or, they will refer to the idea of a glitch being unintended, and while that is correct, it is not the entire story. You may as well define milk by its color and by it being liquid, but if you came across white, liquid, paint you would not call it milk. You may call it milk-like, but paint is definitely not milk. You may distinguish milk further by saying that milk is able to be consumed while paint is not, but then you will have to be later forced to further refine your definition when you come across coconut juice. If you define a thing by its qualities, such as for the glitch: flaw, unintended, in-game; you will have to keep refining the definition to match the thing. It would be better to define a thing by its substance rather than by its qualities. This leads onto the second fact: that a glitch is a thing that exists despite the definitions we put on it. In other words, the substance and essence of what makes a glitch a glitch remains no matter how we define it. The best that we can do is define it by its qualities, which is perfectly alright as long as you admit that the thing exists independent of its definition. The only reason why I mention this at all is because I have noticed a particular tendency of people to justify the use of ‘fastflags exploitation’ by referring to it as glitching. In other words, they appear to believe that because it is a glitch, and because it is not an exploit, that it must be ethical, or at the very least no more unethical than glitching. I will make clear later on why it being a glitch actually has no bearing on whether it is ethical at all, however for now I will content myself with explaining why the fastflags exploit is not a glitch any more than it is an exploit.
</h3>

<h3 align="center">There was a game I once played called Rise of Nations. I would often find myself going into the files of that game and editing the values there in order to make my own version of Rise of Nations. This is what is called ‘modification’. “A modification, often abbreviated to mod, is an alteration or creation of files for a game engine, which allow it to modify the gameplay style, graphics, environments, models, etc.” (Valve Developer Community) This, in a sense, is precisely what is being done with fastflags exploitation. The only difference is that most legal modification does not allow for what we see happening in games with players who exploit fastflags, and that the abilities given by fastflags exploitation is not intended as it is in some games. To call the ‘fastflags exploit’ a glitch merely because it uses a flaw from developers is ridiculous. We intuitively know without our absurd attempts to justify by denotations, selective definitions, and overextended partial definitions, that the fastflags exploit is not a glitch. It is ‘modification’ (AKA ‘modding’), it is not ‘glitching’. To say that the ‘fastflags exploit’ is a glitch is like calling coconut juice milk when it clearly tastes differently.
</h3>

<h3 align="center">If this is insufficient for you, and you still believe that the fastflags exploit is a glitch, then there is another way that I have come to look at which may be useful for you. I was conversing with Alexkaboom when he told me that glitches are the most natural of the cheats, and exploits (DDL injection) the least natural. Another way you could see it is that glitches are the most natural and exploits the most artificial. Correspondingly, you could see it as this: the field of glitches is a realm of discovery, which is in contrast with exploits which is a realm of creation. The exploiter forces their imagination and their will upon the game for their own advantage, whereas the glitcher is bound by the developer’s imagination and will, forced to obey its physics. A glitcher does not break physics or change physics, they merely use physics with all its flaws and features for their own advantage. In that sense, that makes a glitcher very much like a discoverer, an explorer of Roblox’s physics much like a scientist is an explorer of this Reality’s physics. An exploiter is like an artist, a creator, but the problem is not that they are an artist or a creator, but that they are forcing their imagination and their will upon a game that is not their creation. Modification in general is more towards the artificial side, but normally it is not unethical as exploiting clearly is. However, with Roblox, and most especially with the fastflags exploit, it is both natural and artificial because on one hand, the person who uses the fastflags exploit is not creating a loophole, but using a loophole which already existed. In other words, it was discovered not created. On the other hand, although the loophole already existed, the folder and the file within the folder did not already exist and required that someone created them. Finally, just like an exploiter forces their will upon the game for their advantage, so too does the exploiter of fastflags, as they edit values and change the very physics of their client. If glitching is truly a realm of discovery, then never could it be that the editing of values and the changing of physics would be glitching except under special circumstances which will likely never happen. From the lens of natural vs artificial, it does not make sense that the fastflags exploit would be a glitch.
</h3>

<h3 align="center">I am of the belief that ethics does not care for what a thing is, but instead what that thing is used for. For instance, a knife is not in itself ethical or unethical, good or evil, but the action of murder which can come from the use of that knife is unethical. In the same way, I do not think that ethics cares if we are evil with glitches, with exploits, with fastflags, or without any cheats. As to what exactly makes an action ethical or unethical in a video game, you merely need to use your own intuition, your own observations, and your own experiences playing video games. Being only a player of Prison Life, an experience of Roblox, I can only speak on ethics in terms of that game, however I believe that there are concepts to ethics in Prison Life which can be extended universally not just to all experiences of Roblox, but all video games. In Prison Life, there are a few glitch spots, which when glitched into can allow for the player to shoot through the wall. Just by intuition, a Prison Life player seeing a glitcher shooting through the wall would understand that it is unethical. Similarly, in Prison Life, when an exploiter loopkills an entire server, a player who is a victim can tell quite easily that the exploiter’s actions are unethical. I have recently written another paper describing my theories of how ethics can be reasoned instead of merely intuited by observation, however I do not wish to go into it here. The entire point is that just by virtue of something being a glitch does not make a thing right or ethical or moral to do. The same goes with exploits and with modification. However, there are certain points which should be understood: some things are inevitably used unethically. Anyone who plays Prison Life knows that an exploiter will inevitably abuse their power. Here is another point: just because glitches or exploits are harmless in one particular experience of Roblox does not mean that they are harmless in every experience. For instance, if you could increase your speed by a little enough not to be detected yet enough still to have an advantage, you would have an unfair advantage in some obbies and speedrunning games. These two points are essential to note, because even though there are games where fflags might make no difference whatsoever, that does not make fflags alright to use in any game, just in that game where no difference is made whatsoever. I saw someone make this claim and felt obliged to correct it here. In addition to this, just because fflags is able to be used ethically, does not mean that you should use it. The more power that a thing gives you, the less that you can be trusted with it to use ethically; that’s just fact. The wise thing to do would be to never permit yourself to have access to that power, even if you believe you have the ability to not abuse it. It is my opinion that fflags is one such thing that is inevitably abused, at least where it is able to be abused. Obviously if there is a game where there is no need for a concept of fairness, then I see no reason why fflags can’t be used. But in Prison Life it does matter, and likely in many other games as well. The best thing to do would be to refrain from creating a folder called ClientSettings at all cost.
</h3>
